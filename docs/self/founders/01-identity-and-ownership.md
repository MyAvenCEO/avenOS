# Your identity, your data

Most software treats your identity as an entry in a database somewhere else. You sign up, a server creates an account for you, and from then on every action you take has to be approved by that server. If the server goes down, you lose access. If the company that runs it changes its mind, you lose access. If the database is breached, the part of you that lives there — your password, your records, your messages — gets copied.

AvenOS works differently. Your identity here is not stored on a server. It is generated by your Mac, the first time you open the app, and it lives inside a small chip called the Secure Enclave that exists specifically to guard secrets. The private part of that identity — the part that proves you are you — never leaves the chip. Not to Apple, not to us, not to anyone. There is no account database to breach because there is no account database.

When you press Touch ID to unlock the app, your Mac is not phoning home to verify you. It is asking the Secure Enclave, locally, to authorize a cryptographic operation. The chip checks your fingerprint against a template that was stored when you set up Touch ID, and if it matches, the operation proceeds. You are authenticating to your own hardware, not to a remote service.

This shifts what ownership means. Your data is yours because the keys that protect it are yours, in a way that no software policy can take back. If something happens to AvenOS as a company — if we vanish, if the servers shut down, if you simply decide you want to leave — your data on your machine remains accessible to you. Nothing has to grant you permission, because permission was always inherent in the hardware.

Other devices on your network work the same way. Each one generates its own identity in its own Secure Enclave. They recognize each other not through a central authority but through cryptographic signatures: when your iPhone receives a message signed by your Mac, it can verify mathematically that the signature came from a key only your Mac possesses. No middleman needs to confirm the relationship.

What you trade away in exchange is the safety net of an account you can reset by clicking "Forgot password?". There is no such mechanism here, because there is no central authority to grant you a new key. If you lose your Mac without having paired another device, your identity on this network is gone. That feels like a downside, and in some ways it is — but it is also the same property that makes the system worth trusting in the first place. Nobody can lock you out, because nobody is in the loop to make that decision. The price of self-sovereign identity is that the responsibility is also yours.

The developers section that follows goes deep on exactly how each piece works — the chip, the keys, the derivation, the signatures. The takeaway here is simpler than any of that: your identity in AvenOS is a property of your hardware, and your data is yours because the cryptography backing it is yours, not borrowed from someone else's server.
