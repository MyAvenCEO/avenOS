# Storytelling — the Story Finder ("want · but · until")

Maybe you had an idea for a video. Maybe it's been sitting in your notes for
months. Or maybe you've already shot the footage — and now you're just staring
at it, because somewhere between the idea and the final video you don't know what
this video should be anymore.

The idea was never really the problem. The problem is there's **no story**.

There's a big difference between a **video** and a **story**, and it's often the
reason someone either clicks off, or keeps watching and actually *feels*
something. This skill gets you from one to the other. It's surprisingly simple —
**three steps** — and once you understand it you can look at any idea and
immediately know *what* your video actually is.

## How to run it (interactively)

When the user brings an idea/topic/footage, walk them through the questions
**one at a time**, in order. Don't dump all the theory — ask, listen, validate,
dig deeper when an answer is weak, then produce the one sentence. Treat their
answers like the Story Finder tool does: short questions, honest feedback,
refine at the end.

---

## The one question that comes first: **What do we want?**

Before the three steps — before anything — there's one question that already
changes how you look at the idea:

> **What do we want?**

We have to know what the idea is moving *towards*. Ask: *what do you — and most
of the time, your audience — want from it?* You only need **one line**.

- This video → "We want to turn our ideas into videos people actually watch."
- A personal first-YouTube-video → "I want to become a YouTuber."
- A cooking video → "I want to cook the perfect pasta."

Keep it to a single line of what you want. **Everything else — the reason to keep
watching — flows from what we actually want.**

---

## Step 1 — Find the **tension**

Once you know what you want, the first thing to look for is **tension**.

That feeling when you're watching a video and something just keeps you from
clicking away, even if you're not sure why — most of the time that's tension
doing its job. It's the feeling that **something isn't resolved yet, but you want
to see it resolved**, so you really want to know how it ends.

Tension can come from two places:

- **Internal** — the obstacle *inside* you. Fear, doubt, an uncomfortable feeling
  holding you back. (Wanting to start your channel, but every time you sit down to
  film, the fear pulls you back.)
- **External** — the obstacle in the *world around* you. (A hiker; the weather is
  turning and they're not sure they'll make the top. The pasta that keeps sticking
  together and you can't figure out why.)

It can be **internal, external, or both** — and often one tension creates the
other. Lean into whichever matters most.

**If an idea ever felt interesting, there's almost always some tension hiding
inside it** — sometimes you just have to dig a little deeper to find it (see
"Digging deeper" below). Without tension you still have a **topic**, not a story.

> Note — tension isn't always struggle. For wedding films or after-movies it's
> often **anticipation**: the whole day builds toward one moment everyone is
> waiting for — something that hasn't happened yet. That's tension too. Just as
> powerful as the "negative" kind.

---

## Step 2 — Find the **change**

Tension alone doesn't make a story. **Something has to be different at the end
than at the beginning.** Like tension, change can be internal or external:

- **Internal change** — something shifts *within* the person: a realization that
  lands, a fear that gets faced. (Someone postponing their first video for years
  — who finally creates it and posts it.)
- **External change** — something in the *world* visibly transforms. (The same
  hiker now standing at the top; the view is completely different. The pasta that
  kept sticking now comes out right.)

Get one clear answer: **What changed by the end — internal or external?**

If you can answer that, combined with the tension, **you already have the core of
your story.**

---

## Step 3 — Write it in **one sentence** (the compass)

Write the whole thing down in **one sentence** that captures the core. It follows
a simple logic:

> **want · but · until** — "I wanted **[want]**, but **[tension]**, until **[change]**."

Examples:

- Hiker: *"I wanted to reach the top of the mountain, but the weather suddenly
  changed and I wasn't sure I'd make it, until I just kept going through the storm
  and made it to the top."*
- Pasta: *"I wanted to cook the perfect pasta, but it kept sticking together,
  until I learned this trick."*

That sentence is **your compass** — one of the most important pieces of work in
the whole video. When this is clear, you know exactly what the video is trying to
say. And **you will get lost** during the edit (everyone does) — when you do, come
back to this sentence for direction.

---

## Validating answers (give honest feedback, then refine)

After the three answers, check the story and push back when it's weak — exactly
like the tool does:

- **Change too similar to want?** If the "change" basically restates the want
  ("until I just started"), it's not change yet. Ask: *what actually shifted? Did
  you realize something, do something differently, or see it in a new way?*
  - Weak: want = "become a YouTuber", change = "I just started." → same thing.
  - Strong: change = "I realized the only way to beat my self-doubt was to just
    finish one small video first."
- **No tension?** Then it's still a topic. Dig deeper (below).
- When it holds together, produce the **refined one sentence** and offer to tighten
  the wording. Example refined output:
  > *"I wanted to become a YouTuber, but I had a lot of self-doubt and kept
  > getting stuck every time I tried, until I realized the only way to beat my
  > self-doubt was to just finish one small video first."*

---

## Digging deeper (when an idea is "just a topic")

Many ideas fail the test at first — that's normal. Don't force a script out of it.
Dig:

> Ask: **what was really worth remembering?** Maybe something didn't go as
> planned, or there was a moment you felt lost, overwhelmed, or out of your
> comfort zone.

### Worked example — a travel video (topic → fail → story)

- **Idea (topic):** "a video about a food market I visited in Japan."
- **What do we want?** "Find and try the best street food in the market." Clear —
  a good starting point.
- **Tension?** Not really — there's a direction but no tension.
- **Change?** Nothing's really changing. → **Fails the test. Still a topic.** You
  *could* film it (walk the market, show the food) — but it's a tour, not a story.
- **Dig deeper:** "I walked into the market, couldn't read a single sign, couldn't
  speak a word of the language. Every stall looked incredible but I had no idea
  what anything was — part of me just wanted to leave." → That's tension, and
  notice it's **external** (signs, language) *and* **internal** (wanting to turn
  around) at once.
- **Change:** "I decided to just start pointing at things — and ended up eating the
  best thing I've ever tasted, with great people around me."
- **One sentence:** *"I wanted to find the best food in Japan, but not speaking
  the language made it feel impossible, until I just started pointing at things
  and saying yes."*

That's a story to build a travel video around — instead of just a tour of the
market.

---

## The whole method, condensed

1. **What do we want?** — one line of what you (and your audience) want.
2. **Tension** — the unresolved force (internal / external / both; or anticipation).
3. **Change** — what's different by the end (internal / external).
4. **One sentence:** *I wanted [want], but [tension], until [change].* ← your compass.

Not every story needs struggle. The next time an idea feels interesting but you're
stuck, don't force a script — start here: ask what we want, find the tension, find
the change, write the one sentence. Then take that compass into the
[[video-edit]] skill and build the actual video.
